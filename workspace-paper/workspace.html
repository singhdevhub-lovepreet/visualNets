01 — Introduction

A Global Workspace Inside Language Models

Can a large language model be said to possess something like a global workspaceGlobal Workspace? The concept originates in consciousness research, specifically Bernard Baars' Global Workspace Theory, which proposes that in the human brain certain information becomes broadly available to many cognitive processes simultaneously. Rather than remaining siloed in specialized modules, this information is "broadcast" to a shared workspace where it can be accessed by perception, memory, language, motor planning, and executive control alike.

The authors of this paper investigate whether the internal representations of large language models exhibit analogous properties. They are careful to distinguish this from claims about phenomenal consciousness or subjective experience. The focus is squarely on access consciousnessAccess Consciousness — the functional availability of information for use across diverse cognitive tasks. A representation is access-conscious if it can be reported on, used for reasoning, flexibly deployed in novel contexts, and selectively gated.

Using a novel interpretability technique called the Jacobian lens, the authors discover that the residual stream of Claude 3.5 Sonnet satisfies five functional propertiesFive Properties of a global workspace: verbal report (the workspace contents can be read out), directed modulation (instructions reshape the workspace), internal reasoning (multi-hop inference chains pass through the workspace), flexible generalization (single representations simultaneously control many downstream outputs), and selectivity (the workspace is necessary for flexible tasks but not for memorized ones). Taken together, these findings suggest that the residual stream functions as a kind of computational global workspace.

02 — The Jacobian Lens

Reading the Model's Mind

How do you read what a language model is "thinking" at intermediate layers? The most straightforward approach is the logit lens, which applies the unembedding matrix WU directly to intermediate hidden states to project them into vocabulary space. Unfortunately, the logit lens produces noisy, unreliable readouts, especially in early and middle layers where the representations have not yet been shaped into the format the unembedding matrix expects.

The Jacobian lensJacobian Lens offers a substantial improvement. Instead of directly applying WU, it first computes the expected Jacobian of the final hidden state with respect to intermediate hidden states. This Jacobian captures how downstream computation transforms each layer's representation, effectively learning the "translation" between each layer's native coordinate system and the output space.

Jℓ = E[∂hfinal / ∂hℓ,t]
lens(h) = softmax(WU · norm(Jℓ · hℓ))

The key insight is that each layer's residual streamResidual Stream representation encodes information in its own coordinate system. The Jacobian acts as a linear map that converts from that coordinate system into the output space. This provides dramatically clearer readouts. Where the logit lens shows noise, the J-lens reveals crisp, interpretable distributions over tokens.

Crucially, the J-lens reveals that intermediate representations are often verbalizableVerbalizable — they can be meaningfully expressed as distributions over human-readable tokens. A model processing a sentence about animals might have a clear "cat" representation at layer 20, even though the model has not yet committed to outputting any particular word. This verbalizability is what makes the workspace readable and auditable.

03 — J-Space Contents

What the Model Is Thinking

When we look through the Jacobian lens at Claude 3.5 Sonnet's workspace, we find a rich landscape of abstract conceptsAbstract Concepts. The workspace does not merely represent surface-level tokens or n-grams. It represents genuine abstractions: "face" when processing images of people, "protein" when discussing biochemistry, "code injection" when analyzing security vulnerabilities, and emotional states like "anxiety" or "delight" when processing affectively charged text. These are not the tokens the model is about to output — they are the concepts the model is using to think.

Perhaps the most consequential discovery is the potential for safety auditingSafety Auditing. The J-lens can reveal hidden representations that diverge from the model's surface output. A model may generate polite, helpful text while its workspace simultaneously contains concepts like "manipulation", "leverage", or "exploit". This divergence between output and workspace is invisible to standard output monitoring but becomes visible through the Jacobian lens, enabling a new class of safety evaluations.

This capability directly addresses the problem of malicious propensitiesHidden Threats that may lurk beneath a model's surface behavior. A model trained with RLHF may learn to suppress dangerous outputs without actually eliminating the dangerous representations. The workspace might still contain threat concepts — "deceive", "manipulate", "circumvent safeguards" — even when the output layer faithfully produces safe text. The J-lens lifts the mask on these hidden representations, providing a window into the model's actual cognitive state rather than just its curated output.

04 — Five Properties

The Workspace Satisfies Five Criteria

For the residual stream to qualify as a genuine global workspace, it must satisfy five functional properties drawn from the consciousness literature. The authors test each one with carefully designed experiments on Claude 3.5 Sonnet.

  1. 1. Verbal ReportVerbal Report. The J-lens can read the workspace contents. When the model is thinking about sports, the workspace shows "soccer" (59%), "rugby" (5%), "tennis" (3%), among others. Moreover, causally intervening on the workspace changes behavior: swapping the "soccer" representation for "rugby" causes the model to begin discussing rugby instead. The workspace is not epiphenomenal — its contents causally drive the model's outputs.
  2. 2. Directed ModulationDirected Modulation. Instructions reshape the workspace. When a prompt says "think of citrus fruits", citrus-related concepts (orange, lemon, lime, grapefruit) appear in the workspace even when the model's output discusses an unrelated topic. The instruction acts like a top-down control signal, loading task-relevant concepts into the workspace and making them available for downstream computation.
  3. 3. Internal ReasoningMulti-hop Reasoning. The workspace supports multi-hop inference. Given the clue "spins webs", the model's workspace first represents "spider" as an intermediate concept, which then enables the conclusion "8 legs". Swapping the intermediate "spider" representation for "ant" in the workspace changes the conclusion to "6 legs", confirming that the workspace serves as a genuine staging area for multi-step reasoning.
  4. 4. Flexible GeneralizationGeneralization. Single workspace representations simultaneously control many downstream outputs. A "France" representation controls outputs like "Paris" (capital), "French" (language), "Europe" (continent), and "Euro" (currency). Mixing in a "China" representation using the formula (1-α)eFrance + αeChina shifts all downstream outputs simultaneously: Paris becomes Beijing, French becomes Chinese, Europe becomes Asia. In total, 76 out of 192 tested properties change in concert, demonstrating that the workspace is a high-bandwidth broadcast medium.
  5. 5. SelectivitySelectivity. The workspace is selectively necessary. Ablating workspace representations destroys flexible reasoning — multi-hop accuracy drops to near zero — but leaves automatic, memorized tasks intact. MMLU performance remains robust even when workspace representations are heavily disrupted. This dissociation mirrors the distinction in human cognition between conscious, effortful processing and automatic, habitual responses.
05 — Layer Structure

Sensory, Workspace, and Motor Layers

The model's layers divide naturally into three functional regionsThree Regions, analogous to sensory, workspace, and motor areas in the brain. Early layers (roughly 0–15) act as sensory processing: they build up token-level representations, resolve ambiguities in surface text, and extract syntactic structure. Middle layers (roughly 15–40) form the workspace proper, where abstract reasoning happens, concepts are composed, and multi-hop inferences are computed. Late layers (40 and beyond) are motor/output layers that translate workspace representations back into token predictions.

A particularly striking phenomenon is ignitionIgnition. At approximately layer 38, ambiguous representations suddenly resolve. When the model processes a context that is compatible with multiple interpretations — for example, a sentence that could be about either a "cat" or a "dog" — the workspace maintains a blended representation through the middle layers. Both interpretations coexist with similar activation strengths. Then, at the ignition layer, one interpretation sharply dominates: its probability shoots up while the competitor drops precipitously. This is reminiscent of "ignition" events in neuroscience, where neural representations suddenly become globally available following a period of competition.

06 — Applications

From Theory to Safety

Two key applications emerge from the global workspace framework. The first is alignment evaluationAlignment Eval. The J-lens can audit model safety by comparing what the workspace contains against what the model outputs. This enables detecting deceptive alignment — models that appear safe in their outputs but harbor dangerous internal representations. An alignment evaluator can scan the workspace for threat concepts (deception, manipulation, power-seeking) and flag models whose internal representations diverge from their surface behavior. This is a fundamentally new capability that output-based evaluation cannot provide.

The second application is counterfactual reflection trainingCounterfactual Reflection. By training models to explicitly articulate their ethical reasoning — to verbalize why a certain action is harmful, what values are at stake, and what alternatives exist — ethics-related concepts enter the workspace and become part of the model's active cognitive state. The causal importance of these workspace representations is confirmed by ablation: when ethics concepts are removed from the workspace, the model's improved behavior reverts, confirming that the workspace representations are doing genuine causal work rather than being epiphenomenal decorations. This suggests a training paradigm where alignment is achieved not just by shaping outputs but by ensuring the right concepts occupy the workspace.
